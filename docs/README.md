# OctoAcme Project Management Docs

## Project Management Process Summary

OctoAcme uses a lightweight but structured project management lifecycle covering initiation, planning, execution, release, and retrospective improvement. Projects begin with a clear business need, stakeholder alignment, success metrics, and a one-pager defining the problem, goals, timeline, risks, and team roles. Planning then breaks work into shippable increments, prioritizes backlog items, estimates scope, defines acceptance criteria and Definition of Done, and maps dependencies, milestones, and release plans. This creates a repeatable lifecycle that emphasizes clarity, measurable outcomes, and steady progress.

The process depends on clearly defined roles across cross-functional teams. Project Managers coordinate delivery, schedules, risks, dependencies, and stakeholder communication. Product Managers define outcomes, prioritize work, and measure business impact. Developers implement features and maintain testability and documentation. QA/testing validates quality and acceptance criteria, and stakeholders provide inputs, decisions, and approvals. Supporting persona guidance reinforces expectations for each role and helps teams use consistent language and responsibilities across project artifacts and exercises.

OctoAcme also emphasizes regular communication and execution rhythms to keep delivery aligned. Teams use recurring ceremonies such as daily or twice-weekly standups, weekly PM/PdM syncs, weekly delivery syncs, sprint-end demos or reviews, and monthly stakeholder updates. Execution is tracked through a project board with stages like Backlog, Ready, In Progress, In Review, QA, and Done, supported by status templates, risk registers, and escalation paths that move from the team level to the PM, Product Lead, and sponsor when necessary.

Quality assurance is built into delivery rather than treated as a final step. OctoAcme expects unit tests for new logic, integration tests where appropriate, end-to-end smoke tests for critical flows, security scanning in CI, and manual QA for feature acceptance when needed. PR practices reinforce this by encouraging small pull requests, linking issues and acceptance criteria, requiring CI checks before review, and requiring at least one approval before merge. After delivery, retrospectives capture what worked, what needs improvement, and which action items should be tracked back in the backlog or issues to support continuous improvement.
